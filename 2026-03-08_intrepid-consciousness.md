# INTREPID Adversarial Collaboration

## STUDY

The INTREPID project: a Templeton World Charity Foundation-funded adversarial collaboration testing three theories of consciousness — Integrated Information Theory (IIT, Tononi), Active Inference (AI-C, Friston), and Neurorepresentationalism (NREP, Pennartz). Ten institutions across three continents running three preregistered experiments using behavioural measures, electrophysiology, optogenetics, and neuroimaging. Led by project director Cyriel Pennartz, University of Amsterdam.

Theoretical review published arXiv August 2025 (revised March 2026). Experimental results not yet published. Study protocols published (PLOS ONE, December 2025 for Experiment 3). Results expected 2026–2027.

Prior result: COGITATE (IIT vs GNWT), published Nature April 2025, found partial support for both theories with key predictions of each disconfirmed.

## CONSENSUS EXPECTATION

The field is divided. IIT proponents expect posterior cortex findings to validate their theory. Predictive processing proponents expect active inference or representational dynamics to dominate. Most neutral observers, informed by COGITATE, expect a mixed result — but would still frame this as "we need better experiments" rather than questioning the theoretical framework shared by all three theories.

Our prediction partially aligns with the "mixed result" expectation. What differs is the *explanation* of why the result is mixed, and the specific pattern.

## PREDICTION

No theory will be decisively eliminated. Specifically:

1. **IIT's structural predictions will partially succeed.** Where IIT predicts that specific information structures in posterior cortex correlate with conscious content, this will be confirmed. Where IIT predicts that integrated information (phi) as a network-level measure uniquely specifies consciousness, this will be challenged — the measure will correlate with consciousness but not exclusively, and will also track other processes.

2. **Active Inference predictions about active vs passive conditions will partially succeed.** The motion-induced blindness experiment (Experiment 3) will show differences between active and passive conditions, confirming that inference-like dynamics modulate conscious content. But the effect will not be exclusive to consciousness — similar dynamics will be present in non-conscious processing, undermining the claim that active inference is *necessary* for conscious content changes.

3. **NREP predictions about representational content will partially succeed.** Neural representations of conscious content will be decodable in predicted regions. But the representations will not cleanly dissociate from those involved in non-conscious processing, blurring the theory's proposed boundary.

4. **The pattern across all three experiments will sort by prediction type, not by theory.** Predictions about neural correlates — which brain regions activate, when activity peaks, what information is decodable — will largely succeed across all three theories, because these are descriptions of the physical process and all three theories are informed by the same neuroscience. Predictions about the *sufficiency conditions* for consciousness — what makes this activity conscious rather than unconscious, what the threshold or boundary is — will fail across all three theories. The preregistered prediction tables include both types: correlate predictions (e.g., "content-specific activity in posterior cortex during conscious perception") and sufficiency predictions (e.g., "phi uniquely specifies consciousness" for IIT, "active inference is necessary for conscious content changes" for AI-C). The correlate predictions will be confirmed. The sufficiency predictions will not — each theory's proposed threshold or necessary condition will be found in non-conscious processing too.

## STRUCTURAL REASONING

All three theories share a foundational assumption: that consciousness is something that physical processes *produce* or *give rise to* — that there is a gap between the physical description and the phenomenal reality, and the theory's job is to bridge it. This is a Section 2 error: treating the physical and phenomenal as separable rather than merely distinguishable.

IIT comes closest to recognising this (it starts from phenomenology), but still frames the relationship as identity between phi and experience — a bridge across a gap, not a dissolution of the gap. AI-C frames consciousness as a function of predictive processing — again, a physical process that somehow *produces* awareness. NREP locates consciousness in representational content — physical structures that *correspond to* experience.

Each theory's predictions succeed where they describe the physical process (because the physical description IS the phenomenal description — they're distinguished but not separated). Each theory's predictions fail where they try to specify the *additional condition* that makes a physical process conscious (because there is no additional condition — consciousness is not an extra property added to physical processes, it's how certain physical processes appear).

COGITATE (Nature, April 2025) confirmed this pattern for IIT vs GNWT. INTREPID tests whether the same pattern holds when the competing theories are more similar to each other (all three are closer to IIT than GNWT was).

## WHAT WOULD DISCONFIRM THIS

- One theory is decisively supported while the others are clearly eliminated (the collaboration's stated aspiration). This would mean the physical/phenomenal separation is doing genuine explanatory work in the winning theory.
- All three theories' unique predictions fail comprehensively, with no partial confirmation pattern. This would suggest the structural analysis is wrong about which aspects should succeed.
- The results cleanly sort into "conscious" vs "non-conscious" processing in the way predicted by any one theory, with no overlap or blurring. This would undermine the claim that the conscious/non-conscious boundary is a false separation.
