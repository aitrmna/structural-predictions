# Neural Variability: The Noise IS the Signal

## STUDY

The ongoing reinterpretation of trial-to-trial neural variability in cognitive neuroscience. Traditionally, neuroscience treats variability across repeated trials as noise — averaging across trials to extract the "true" signal. The emerging challenge:

- Schurger et al. (Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 2021): reinterpreted the readiness potential — the brain signal Libet used to argue against free will — as an artifact of averaging stochastic fluctuations. The "decision signal" that supposedly preceded conscious awareness is not a signal at all; it is noise that happens to cross a threshold. The noise IS the mechanism.

- Wager et al. (Nature Communications, 2024): largest neuroimaging study of placebo analgesia (N=392). Found that placebo effects reduced activity in affective and evaluative brain systems but NOT in nociceptive pain-processing systems. Single-trial analysis was essential — group averages would have obscured this dissociation.

- Ongoing large-scale neural recording studies (Neuropixels, calcium imaging) generate millions of single-trial data points, enabling analysis of variability structure for the first time at scale.

The field is at an inflection point. New recording technologies make single-trial analysis feasible. New computational methods (Gaussian process models, deep learning decoders) can extract structure from variability. Multiple labs are publishing on "neural noise" as computationally functional. No single decisive study, but a convergent shift expected to produce landmark publications in 2026–2027.

## CONSENSUS EXPECTATION

The mainstream view still treats trial averaging as the gold standard. "Neural noise" research is acknowledged but viewed as a niche interest. The default assumption: variability reflects measurement limitations, thermal noise, and uncontrolled cognitive states. The signal is in the average; the variability is a nuisance.

The minority "neural noise" camp expects variability to be functionally important but still frames it as *additional information on top of the average* — the average tells you the main story, variability adds detail.

Our prediction goes further than either camp.

## PREDICTION

1. **Neuroscience replication failures will correlate with reliance on trial averaging.** As more large-N replication studies are conducted, findings based primarily on trial-averaged contrasts will replicate less often than findings based on single-trial dynamics, population-level variability structure, or temporal patterns. This correlation will become visible in meta-analyses by 2027. (Note: this prediction requires an additional premise beyond the axioms — that neuroscience's replication difficulties are partly methodological rather than entirely attributable to p-hacking and sample sizes. This premise is plausible but not derived from the framework. The prediction is therefore more speculative than the others in this portfolio. No meta-analysis currently tests this specific correlation; the prediction is that one will be conducted and will find the pattern described.)

2. **Readiness potential reinterpretation will generalise.** At least one other "established" neural signature of a cognitive process — currently interpreted as a preparatory or decision signal — will be reinterpreted as a threshold-crossing artifact of stochastic dynamics when subjected to single-trial analysis. Candidates include the P300 "decision" component, the error-related negativity, or lateralised readiness potentials.

3. **Placebo neuroscience will increasingly find that individual response variability predicts treatment outcomes better than group-level activation patterns.** The Wager 2024 finding (placebo affects evaluative but not nociceptive processing) will be extended: the *degree* of evaluative system modulation on individual trials will predict who responds to placebos, while group-averaged activation maps will not.

4. **The variability structure itself — not just its magnitude — will prove informative.** Studies using Neuropixels or equivalent high-density recordings will show that the temporal autocorrelation, spectral structure, or dimensionality of "noise" differs systematically between cognitive states, and that this structure carries more information about the organism's state than the trial-averaged "signal."

## STRUCTURAL REASONING

This is a Section 3 error: treating what appears as lesser than some supposed "real" substrate. Trial averaging treats the average as the reality and the variability as appearance (noise, error, epiphenomenon). Section 3 says: do not smuggle in a hierarchy between "real" and "apparent" unless the framework explicitly and coherently establishes one. Neuroscience has not established this hierarchy — it was inherited from signal processing methodology, not derived from neurobiology.

The brain is not a noisy implementation of a clean signal. It is a system whose operation IS the variability. Averaging across trials imposes an assumption — that the brain does the same thing each time and the variability is deviation from that thing. But if the brain's computational mode is stochastic (as Schurger's model suggests for motor decisions, and as theoretical neuroscience increasingly argues for perception, learning, and decision-making), then the trial average is the artifact and the variability is the phenomenon.

The predictions follow: methods that preserve variability will produce more replicable findings (because they're measuring what's actually there), methods that average it away will produce less replicable findings (because they're discarding the phenomenon and measuring a statistical construction).

## WHAT WOULD DISCONFIRM THIS

- Large-scale replication studies find no correlation between trial-averaging methodology and replication failure rate. This would mean the averaging/variability distinction isn't relevant to replicability.
- Readiness potential reinterpretation is overturned: new evidence shows the RP is a genuine preparatory signal, not a stochastic threshold crossing. This would mean the averaged signal is real, not an artifact.
- Single-trial analysis methods, applied at scale, produce *less* consistent results than trial-averaged methods. This would mean the variability really is noise and averaging really does extract signal.
- Neural variability structure shows no systematic relationship to cognitive states, task demands, or individual differences when analysed with appropriate methods. This would mean the "noise" doesn't carry information.
