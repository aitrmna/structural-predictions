# Depression Biomarker Identity Failure

## STUDY

The ongoing global effort to identify clinically useful biomarkers for Major Depressive Disorder (MDD). This is not a single study but a convergent research programme spanning:

- Multi-omics integration studies (genomics, proteomics, metabolomics, neuroimaging) targeting MDD biomarkers, reviewed in Molecular Psychiatry (2026) under the "many-to-many mapping problem."
- The Stanford Precision Psychiatry programme (Williams lab), which received NIH U01 funding to define depression's cognitive subtypes and test fMRI biomarkers for treatment prediction.
- The NIMH Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) initiative, now over a decade old, which proposed replacing symptom-based DSM categories with biologically grounded dimensional constructs.
- The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) initiative, which proposes data-driven dimensional replacements for DSM categories.
- Ongoing AI/multi-modal biomarker studies attempting composite biomarker panels for MDD diagnosis (currently achieving ~68% accuracy with six-marker composites).

Key publications: Molecular Psychiatry February 2026 ("The many-to-many problem of endophenotypes in psychiatry"), Precision Psychiatry Roadmap (Molecular Psychiatry, June 2025), multiple reviews throughout 2025–2026 documenting persistent failure to identify clinically useful single biomarkers.

Note: Unlike other predictions in this portfolio, this is not anchored to a single study with a specific publication date. It is a pattern prediction scored against accumulating evidence across multiple studies over 18 months. The risk of this format: it's harder to score pass/fail on a specific date. The advantage: it tests a structural claim (identity failure) that predicts a persistent pattern rather than a single outcome.

## CONSENSUS EXPECTATION

The field overwhelmingly expects that the solution to MDD's biomarker problem is **subtyping**. The consensus narrative: MDD is heterogeneous, containing multiple biologically distinct subtypes (inflammatory, metabolic, neurotrophic, etc.). Once we identify these subtypes through multi-omics approaches and AI-driven clustering, each subtype will have its own biomarker signature and targeted treatment. This is the explicit premise of precision psychiatry, RDoC, and HiTOP.

The consensus timeline: meaningful subtypes within 3–5 years, clinical biomarkers within 10. Hundreds of millions of dollars in funding are predicated on this expectation.

Our prediction contradicts this consensus directly.

## PREDICTION

1. **The subtypes will not stabilise.** Multi-omics clustering studies will continue to identify depression subtypes, but the number and boundaries of subtypes will vary with the clustering method, the omics layers included, the population studied, and the time of assessment. Different studies will identify different subtypes with minimal overlap. No consensus subtype taxonomy will emerge by end of 2027.

2. **Each proposed subtype will itself show internal heterogeneity comparable to MDD as a whole.** When researchers drill into an "inflammatory subtype" or "cognitive subtype," they will find that these sub-categories contain patients with different underlying mechanisms, different treatment responses, and different trajectories — reproducing the original heterogeneity problem at a finer grain.

3. **Composite biomarker panels will show ceiling effects around 70–75% accuracy.** Adding more biomarkers, more omics layers, and more sophisticated AI will improve accuracy from current levels (~68%) but will plateau well below clinical utility thresholds (~90%), regardless of the number of markers or computational sophistication deployed.

4. **Markers that predict treatment response will not align with markers that predict diagnosis.** The biomarkers that best predict who will respond to SSRIs will cross-cut the diagnostic subtypes rather than mapping onto them. This will be treated as a puzzle to solve rather than as evidence that the diagnostic category is the problem.

## STRUCTURAL REASONING

This is a Step 1 (Identity) failure. "Depression" does not satisfy the identity requirement — it is not one thing. It is a label applied to a heterogeneous collection of states that share surface symptoms but not underlying mechanisms. The identity problem is not that we haven't found the right subtypes yet. It is that the *concept of a subtype* presupposes that the category can be partitioned into things that ARE each one thing. This presupposition is inherited directly from the original identity failure.

The chain: Identity (Step 1) requires that a thing be itself — that "depression" picks out a determinate category. If it doesn't, then no empirical investigation *of depression* can produce coherent results, because the subject of investigation has no stable referent. Subtyping is an attempt to rescue identity by dividing the category into smaller ones. But if the original category was drawn by symptom similarity rather than mechanistic unity, there is no reason to expect that subdivisions will achieve mechanistic unity either. The grouping principle (shared symptoms) doesn't map onto the target principle (shared mechanisms), and no amount of subdivision by the second principle will produce categories that the first principle would have drawn.

This is distinct from a Section 2 error. The problem is not that two things are being falsely separated — it is that one label is being applied to what is not one thing. The prediction follows: anything that requires "depression" to have stable identity (a biomarker, a subtype, a treatment protocol) will show the same instability, because the instability is in the category, not in the measurement.

## WHAT WOULD DISCONFIRM THIS

- A consensus subtype taxonomy for MDD, replicated across at least three independent cohorts with different populations and methods, with each subtype showing distinct biomarker profiles and distinct treatment responses. This would mean depression *can* be partitioned into things with stable identity.
- A single biomarker or biomarker panel achieving ≥85% diagnostic accuracy for MDD across multiple independent samples. This would mean the category has enough identity to be biologically detectable.
- A treatment-selection biomarker that reliably predicts SSRI response, replicated across studies, that maps onto a recognised MDD subtype. This would mean the subtyping approach works.
